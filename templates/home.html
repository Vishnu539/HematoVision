Types of Blood Cells

Eosinophil

Eosinophils are white blood cells involved in allergic responses and defense against parasitic infections. They release enzymes that combat invaders and are often elevated in asthma, hay fever, and autoimmune conditions. Their granules stain reddish-pink under the microscope, making them easily recognizable.

Lymphocyte

Lymphocytes play a vital role in the immune response. They include B cells (produce antibodies), T cells (destroy infected cells), and natural killer cells. Elevated lymphocytes can indicate viral infections or immune system disorders. They have a round nucleus and sparse cytoplasm.

Monocyte

Monocytes are the largest white blood cells and are essential for clearing dead cells and pathogens. They migrate into tissues and become macrophages or dendritic cells, helping in tissue repair and antigen presentation. Increased counts may signal chronic infections or inflammation.

Neutrophil

Neutrophils are first responders to bacterial infections. They kill invaders by engulfing them (phagocytosis) and releasing enzymes. Their numbers rise during acute infections and inflammation. They have a multi-lobed nucleus and pale pink cytoplasm.
